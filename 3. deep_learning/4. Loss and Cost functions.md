### Math calc

now we want to calculate how wrong the model is (loss).

to get the loss we need to know the difference between the predictions we made and the actual y (the target) and square them to remove any negative values.
previously in a single neuron model we had a loss function that depended on the $y$ and $A$ 

$$L(\theta) = (y-A)^2$$

but now we want to get a loss function that incorporates the $y$ and $A$ for a whole layer.

the approach we will be taking here is simply summing the loss for a single sample across all the output neurons.

so for a single sample of the data-set:

$$L^j(\theta, X) = \sum_{\text{neurons}} (y^j_i - a_i^j)^2$$

- super-script ($j$) $\rightarrow$ refers to some sample in the date-set
- sub-script ($i) $\rightarrow$ refers to which neuron we talking about

and the cost function is simply the mean of this loss function for all the data samples:

$$L(\theta,X) = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{j=1}^{m} L^j(\theta, X)$$

this method is called the mean squared error (MSE).

### Vectorization

To free our brains from the pain of writing loops or complicated element-by-element tracking, we can vectorize our operations. This allows us to think in unified matrices and vectors instead of isolated data samples and neurons.

In a multi-neuron output layer, the activation outputs and the ground-truth targets are no longer single-column vectors like they were in a single-neuron model. Instead, they expand into a matrix grid of shape $(m \times h)$:

$$
\underbrace{A^{[L]}}_{(m \times h)} = \begin{bmatrix} 
a_{11} & a_{12} & \dots & a_{1h} \\ 
a_{21} & a_{22} & \dots & a_{2h} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 
a_{m1} & a_{m2} & \dots & a_{mh} 
\end{bmatrix}
, \quad \underbrace{Y}_{(m \times h)} = \begin{bmatrix}
y_{11} & y_{12} & \dots & y_{1h} \\ 
y_{21} & y_{22} & \dots & y_{2h} \\ 
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 
y_{m1} & y_{m2} & \dots & y_{mh} 
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Where:
* $m \rightarrow$ is the number of training samples (batch size)
* $h \rightarrow$ is the number of processing neurons (nodes) in this final layer

To execute the subtraction and squaring without any loops, we utilize element-wise matrix operations:

$$
(Y - A^{[L]})^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 
(y_{11} - a_{11})^2 & \dots & (y_{1h} - a_{1h})^2 \\ 
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 
(y_{m1} - a_{m1})^2 & \dots & (y_{mh} - a_{mh})^2 
\end{bmatrix}
$$

This matrix maps out every single squared error across all samples for all output nodes simultaneously.

#### Collapsing the Matrix into Cost

Recall our core rule: the loss function for a single sample requires summing all the squared errors over all the output layer nodes. To achieve this across our entire batch matrix without a loop, we sum all values across the **columns** (`axis=1` in code). 

This operation compresses the $(m \times h)$ matrix down into a single-column error vector of shape $(m \times 1)$, where each row contains the total calculated loss for that specific sample.

Finally, the total network **Cost ($J$)** is determined by taking the average (mean) of this column vector, dividing the losses by our total number of samples $m$.

### putting it into coding

```python
import numpy as np 

# y: The true label target matrix, Shape: (m x h) 
# A: The final layer activation matrix from the forward pass, Shape: (m x h) 

# 1. Compute the element-wise squared deviations 
squared_error = (y - A) ** 2 

# 2. Compute the total cost J 
# We sum across axis=1 (columns) to combine individual neuron errors per sample. 
# then we take the mean across the rows to get the average batch cost. 
cost = np.mean(np.sum(squared_error, axis=1))
```
